# GitLab Leadership

## Core Principles

### Managers of One
All team members at GitLab are expected to develop self-leadership skills and set their own priorities to achieve goals. GitLab operates as an all-remote organization, so every person must be capable of directing their own work without constant supervision.

### Direct Communication
Every team member is encouraged to reach out directly to whoever is the correct person to quickly unblock issues. Managers should never be communication bottlenecks. Hierarchical chains of command slow things down and are avoided wherever possible.

### Asynchronous Work
GitLab prioritizes written documentation and asynchronous communication over synchronous meetings. Things need to be written down in issues as they happen. The default is async; synchronous meetings are a last resort.

## Leadership Practices

### Decision-Making
Decisions at GitLab are not consensus-driven. One person decides after gathering input and feedback from relevant stakeholders. This prevents endless deliberation and keeps the organization moving. Disagreement is welcome, but the decision-maker has authority and accountability.

### Feedback Culture
Leaders provide regular, constructive feedback focused on improvement. Feedback is direct, timely, specific, and kind. Avoiding feedback is not kindness — it deprives people of the information they need to grow.

### Building High-Performing Teams
GitLab uses the Drexler-Sibbet model for team development, recognizing seven stages from orientation through renewal. Leaders understand which stage their team is in and adapt their approach accordingly.

### Effective Escalations
When escalating an issue, the escalation should clearly answer:
- Why this issue matters
- Relevant details and context
- What has already been tried
- What is specifically needed to resolve it

## Leadership Development

GitLab invests in developing leaders through:
- **The Elevate program** — dedicated training for remote team leadership
- **Coaching and mentorship** — pairing leaders with experienced coaches
- **Job shadowing programs** — cross-functional exposure
- **LinkedIn Learning and internal platforms** — self-directed management courses

## Foundational Reading

GitLab's leadership philosophy draws heavily from *High Output Management* by Andrew Grove. The core of that philosophy: a manager's output is the output of their team. Leaders succeed when their teams succeed.

Trust, transparency, and efficiency are the foundation of leadership at GitLab. A leader who hoards information, avoids difficult conversations, or optimizes for their own visibility rather than team results is not aligned with GitLab's values.
