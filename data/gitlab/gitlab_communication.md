# GitLab Communication

GitLab's communication practices are designed to support an all-remote, globally distributed workforce across 60+ countries.

## Core Communication Philosophy

GitLab prioritizes **asynchronous communication as a starting point**, using public issues, merge requests, and Slack channels to maintain transparency while accommodating different time zones.

The key escalation rule: **when we go back and forth three times, we jump on a synchronous video call.**

## Effective Communication Guidelines

Eight core principles guide all communication at GitLab:

1. **Assume positive intent** — Interpret messages charitably before reacting
2. **Prioritize kindness** — Be kind in written communication, which lacks tone cues
3. **Express thoughts responsibly and inclusively** — Consider how your words land for a global audience
4. **Own your statements** — Take responsibility for what you say and write
5. **Model company values** — Every interaction is an opportunity to demonstrate GitLab's values
6. **Welcome feedback** — Be open to being corrected or updated
7. **Recognize when 1:1 is more effective** — Some conversations need a direct call
8. **Adhere to anti-harassment policies** — Zero tolerance for harassment in any form

## Communication Channels

Different forums serve different purposes at GitLab:

| Channel | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Handbook** | Documentation, processes, and decisions |
| **Merge Requests** | Proposing specific changes |
| **Issues** | Discussions without a proposed solution yet |
| **Slack** | Quick questions and real-time coordination |
| **Email / Newsletters** | Formal announcements |
| **Meetings** | Complex discussions and time-sensitive decisions |

## Key Practices

### Start with Merge Requests
Default to merge requests over issues when proposing changes. They are more actionable and facilitate focused discussion around a concrete proposal.

### Asynchronous-First Approach
Written communication enables scaling across time zones and creates a searchable, permanent record. Synchronous calls are reserved for situations requiring immediate consensus or when back-and-forth has exceeded three exchanges.

### Public by Default
Use public channels over private messages. This keeps information accessible to the whole team and avoids siloing knowledge. If something can be said in a public channel, it should be.

### Handbook First
When answering a question, if the answer is not already in the handbook, write it there first, then share the link. This builds institutional knowledge rather than repeating answers in chat.

### Bias Toward Clarity
Be direct. Avoid ambiguity. State your ask, your decision, or your concern plainly. Diplomatic vagueness creates confusion in an async environment where you cannot immediately clarify.

### Responsible Use of AI
Team members using generative AI in communication must:
- Verify accuracy of all AI-generated content
- Maintain authenticity — do not let AI speak for you without review
- Own the quality of all output regardless of how it was created

## Meetings

Meetings at GitLab should have:
- A written agenda shared in advance
- A designated facilitator
- Notes documented in a shared location
- Clear action items with owners and due dates

When a meeting ends without documented outcomes, that work is effectively lost. Documentation is not optional — it is part of completing the task.

## Feedback Culture

GitLab encourages direct, timely, and kind feedback. Feedback should be:
- **Specific** — describe the behavior or outcome, not the person
- **Timely** — given close to the event while context is fresh
- **Actionable** — focus on what can be changed
- **Two-way** — both giving and receiving feedback are skills to develop

Avoiding difficult conversations does not align with GitLab's transparency value. Kindness means being honest, not being comfortable.
